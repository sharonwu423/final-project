Six lenses for understanding social behavior.

This page organizes the major topics by concept rather than chapter number. Each section explains how social psychology studies the relationship between individuals, groups, culture, and social situations. Together, these concepts show that behavior is rarely caused by personality alone; it is also shaped by context, expectations, relationships, and social influence.

Genes, Culture, and Gender

This topic examines how human behavior is shaped by both biological influences and cultural expectations. Genes may contribute to certain tendencies, but culture teaches people what behaviors are considered appropriate, valued, or expected. Gender is especially important because societies often attach different roles, norms, and expectations to people based on gender.

Prejudice

Prejudice refers to a negative attitude toward a person or group based on group membership. It is often connected to stereotypes, which are simplified beliefs about groups. Social psychology studies how prejudice develops, how it is maintained by institutions and social norms, and how it can influence judgment even when people do not intend to be unfair.

Aggression

Aggression involves behavior intended to harm another person, either physically, verbally, or socially. It can be influenced by frustration, anger, arousal, media exposure, environmental stress, and group dynamics. This concept helps explain why aggression is not only an individual reaction, but also something shaped by social situations.

Attraction and Intimacy

Attraction is influenced by factors such as proximity, similarity, repeated interaction, and perceived responsiveness. Intimacy develops when people feel understood, trusted, and accepted. Social psychology connects relationships to broader patterns of communication, self-disclosure, attachment, and emotional support.

Helping

Helping behavior, also called prosocial behavior, refers to actions that benefit other people. People may help because of empathy, moral values, social responsibility, or expectations from the situation. The presence of others can sometimes reduce helping because responsibility feels shared, which shows how strongly context affects behavior.

Conflict and Peacemaking

Conflict occurs when individuals or groups perceive their goals, needs, or identities as incompatible. Social psychology studies how competition, misunderstanding, and group identity can intensify conflict. Peacemaking involves strategies such as communication, cooperation, perspective-taking, and shared goals to reduce tension and rebuild trust.

Topics include genes, culture, gender, prejudice, aggression, attraction, intimacy, helping, conflict, and peacemaking.
